\begin{abstract}
When a wind farm is designed in isolation---ignoring the wakes of potential neighboring farms---how much energy is sacrificed?
We define this loss as \emph{design regret}: the difference in annual energy production between a layout re-optimized with knowledge of neighbors and the original isolation-designed layout, both evaluated under the same neighbor wakes.
Using greedy adversarial neighbor placement and converged multistart optimization ($K = 500$ random restarts), we sweep the two-parameter elliptical wind rose space of \citet{Hart2025} and find that design regret varies six-fold across wind climate: from \SI{17}{GWh/yr} (\SI{0.4}{\percent} of AEP) under bidirectional conditions to \SI{101}{GWh/yr} (\SI{2.5}{\percent}) under concentrated unidirectional conditions.
Wind rose directionality---specifically the folding parameter controlling uni- versus bidirectionality---is the dominant driver.
Regret cross-sections at $K = 500$ confirm an ``ambush effect'': peak vulnerability occurs at oblique angles to the dominant wind direction, not along it.
A self-interested neighbor comparison reveals that under concentrated unidirectional wind, a neighbor optimizing its own production causes nearly double the design regret of a fixed reference farm, because it concentrates turbines in the high-wind upwind corridor.
Critically, we find that the computational effort required for reliable regret estimation far exceeds standard practice: five random starts capture only \SIrange{55}{86}{\percent} of the converged regret.
We recommend a minimum of 300 multistart optimizations for any study computing AEP differences between design strategies.
Buffer distance analysis shows that regret decay depends on wind rose shape: concentrated unidirectional sites require buffers exceeding $40D$ (\SI{9.6}{km}), while bidirectional sites need minimal separation.
\end{abstract}


\introduction

The North Sea is entering an era of unprecedented wind farm clustering.
Denmark's Vindm\o{}lleparken energy island will connect up to \SI{10}{GW} across multiple farms in a shared concession zone; the German Bight already hosts over \SI{8}{GW} with plans for \SI{30}{GW} by 2050; and the broader North Sea basin is projected to reach \SI{300}{GW} as part of Europe's \SI{500}{GW} offshore target.
At this density, wind farms no longer operate in isolation.
Cluster wake losses in the German Bight are projected to reduce capacity factors by \SI{30}{\percent}, with half of the loss attributable to cross-border wake accumulation \citep{Fliegner2025}.
The phenomenon---increasingly termed ``wind theft'' \citep{Finseraas2024, Du2026}---has prompted the UK to issue the first national policy guidance on inter-farm wake effects and NYSERDA to recommend minimum inter-farm spacing of \SI{4}{nmi} \citep{NYSERDA2024}.
Wake-induced velocity deficits have been measured at distances exceeding \SI{50}{km} \citep{Schneemann2020, Canadillas2022}, and inter-farm losses are projected to exceed the impact of a century of climate change on North Sea wind resources \citep{Warder2025}.

Yet these emerging policy responses share a common gap: they treat all sites uniformly.
Buffer distance recommendations, ``good neighbour'' design principles, and compensation frameworks do not account for variation in wind climate.
This is a significant omission.
A site where wind blows predominantly from one direction creates a single, predictable wake corridor that a neighbor-aware layout can avoid.
A site with bidirectional wind creates competing corridors that no single rearrangement can escape.
The value of coordinated design---and the cost of failing to coordinate---should depend fundamentally on the directional structure of the wind resource.

We formalize this intuition through the concept of \emph{design regret}: the AEP difference between a layout designed with perfect neighbor foresight and one designed in isolation, both evaluated under the same neighbor wakes:
\begin{equation}\label{eq:regret}
    R = \text{AEP}(\mathbf{x}^*_\text{cons}, \mathbf{n}) - \text{AEP}(\mathbf{x}^*_\text{lib}, \mathbf{n}).
\end{equation}
Design regret isolates the cost of the design decision itself, separate from the total AEP reduction caused by neighbor wakes.
By sweeping the elliptical wind rose parameterization of \citet{Hart2025}---which separates directional concentration from bidirectionality---we quantify how wind rose shape governs design regret.
The answer is dramatic: regret varies six-fold across the shape space, from \SI{17}{GWh/yr} under bidirectional conditions to \SI{101}{GWh/yr} under concentrated unidirectional conditions.
Buffer distance requirements inherit this dependence.

\citet{Quick2026omae} applied this framework to the Danish Energy Island cluster and found negligible design regret for all nine neighbor configurations.
We show this is not a general property of offshore wind, but a consequence of DEI's partially bidirectional wind climate ($f \approx 0.38$).
Sites in the southern North Sea with strongly unidirectional southwesterly winds sit in the high-regret region of the shape space and are substantially more vulnerable to uncoordinated cluster development.

However, accurately computing these quantities reveals a methodological challenge that undermines all existing regret-like calculations.
Layout optimization is highly multimodal \citep{Thomas2023, ValottaRodrigues2024}, and regret---as the \emph{difference} between two optimization outcomes---amplifies the effect of local optima.
We show that five random starts, more than many published studies employ, capture only \SIrange{55}{86}{\percent} of the converged regret.

This paper makes four contributions:
\begin{enumerate}
    \item A convergence study demonstrating that 300+ multistart optimizations are needed for reliable regret estimation---over an order of magnitude more than typical practice.
    \item A systematic sweep of the wind rose shape space showing that design regret varies six-fold with directionality, with concentrated unidirectional sites most vulnerable.
    \item Buffer distance analysis quantifying how neighbor separation reduces regret, and how the decay rate depends on wind rose shape.
    \item Regret cross-sections revealing an ``ambush effect'' in which peak design vulnerability occurs at oblique angles to the dominant wind direction.
\end{enumerate}


\section{Methods}\label{sec:methods}

\subsection{Problem formulation}

We consider a target wind farm of $N_t = 50$ IEA \SI{15}{MW} turbines ($D = \SI{240}{m}$, hub height \SI{150}{m}) within a fixed polygonal boundary derived from the Danish Energy Island concession zone.
Minimum inter-turbine spacing is $4D$ (\SI{960}{m}).
This configuration is representative of near-future North Sea developments: the IEA \SI{15}{MW} turbine is the reference class for planned GW-scale projects, and the DEI polygon provides a realistic, irregular boundary.

The liberal layout $\mathbf{x}^*_\text{lib}$ maximizes AEP assuming no external turbines.
The conservative layout $\mathbf{x}^*_\text{cons}$ maximizes AEP with specified neighbors~$\mathbf{n}$.
Both are evaluated with neighbors present to compute regret via Eq.~\eqref{eq:regret}.
By construction, $R \geq 0$ when both reach global optima.
We enforce this computationally through \emph{regret pooling}: the conservative AEP is taken as $\max(\text{best conservative AEP},\; \text{liberal AEP with neighbors})$, guaranteeing non-negative regret even with imperfect optimization.

\subsection{Wake model}

We use the \citet{Bastankhah2014} Gaussian deficit model with $k = 0.04$ and root-sum-of-squares superposition.
The simulation is implemented in JAX \citep{jax2018github}, enabling automatic differentiation through the wake model.
AEP is computed over 24 equally-spaced wind direction sectors at a constant wind speed of \SI{9}{m/s}:
\begin{equation}
    \text{AEP} = 8760 \sum_{k=1}^{24} w_k \sum_{i=1}^{N_t} P_i(U_{i,k}),
\end{equation}
where $w_k$ is the sector frequency, $P_i$ is the turbine power curve, and $U_{i,k}$ is the effective wind speed at turbine $i$ under direction~$k$.

\subsection{Wind rose parameterization}

Wind rose shape is parameterized using the elliptical model of \citet{Hart2025}:
\begin{itemize}
    \item \textbf{Shape parameter $a$}: controls directional concentration ($a = 1/\sqrt{\pi} \approx 0.564$ gives uniform).
    \item \textbf{Folding parameter $f \in [0,1]$}: controls symmetry ($f = 0$ bidirectional; $f = 1$ unidirectional).
\end{itemize}
The prevailing direction is fixed at \SI{270}{\degree} and wind speed at \SI{9}{m/s}.
We sweep $a \in \{0.3, 0.4, 0.5, 0.6, 0.7, 0.8, 0.9\}$ and $f \in \{0.0, 0.25, 0.5, 0.75, 1.0\}$ (35 combinations).
This range spans the variety of North Sea wind climates, from the strongly unidirectional southwesterlies of the German Bight to the more bidirectional conditions at the Danish Energy Island.

Fitting the DEI wind rose with the elliptical model yields $a \approx 0.64$, $f \approx 0.38$ ($R^2 = 0.84$), placing it in the moderate-concentration, partially-bidirectional regime.

\subsection{Adversarial neighbor placement}

Neighbors are placed using a greedy grid search.
A candidate grid with $5D$ spacing extends $50D$ from the target boundary ($\sim$\num{1567} candidates).
At each of 30 greedy steps, the candidate maximizing incremental regret is selected, each requiring a full $K$-start conservative optimization.
This provides an \emph{upper bound} on design regret: it identifies the most vulnerable directions and the maximum cost of designing in isolation, though it does not represent realistic neighbor behavior.

\subsection{Regret cross-sections}

To map directional vulnerability, a standardized reference neighbor farm ($5 \times 5 = 25$ turbines at $7D$ spacing) is placed at each combination of 24 bearings (\SI{15}{\degree} steps) and 7 distances ($5D$--$60D$), producing 168 evaluations per wind rose.
At each position, the target farm is re-optimized with $K$ restarts.
The resulting polar map---the \emph{regret cross-section}---separates bearing and distance effects and reveals whether vulnerability is isotropic or directionally concentrated.

\subsection{Multistart optimization and convergence}

Each layout optimization uses gradient-based SGD with \num{5000} iterations \citep{Quick2023sgd}.
Restarts include grid initialization, the liberal layout (ensuring regret pooling), and random polygon-sampled positions.
Following the convergence analysis of \citet{Quick2026omae}, who found $\sim$400 starts needed for 66-turbine liberal layout convergence, we perform a dedicated convergence study (Section~\ref{sec:convergence}).

Production configurations: $K = 500$ for the wind rose sweep and buffer analysis; $K = 100$ for cross-sections.

\subsection{Convergence study: how many starts are enough?}\label{sec:convergence}

A $5 \times 5$ reference neighbor farm is placed at a fixed bearing and distance for each.

At $K = 5$, regret captures only \SIrange{55}{86}{\percent} of the converged value.
The moderate bidirectional case is worst: $K = 5$ finds only \SI{55}{\percent} of true regret because the landscape has many local optima with similar standalone AEP but different neighbor-present AEP.
All configurations stabilize by $K = 300$.
SGD iteration convergence is much faster: at $K = 500$, regret at \num{5000} iterations is within \SI{3}{\percent} of the \num{10000}-iteration value.

The root cause is catastrophic cancellation: regret is a \emph{difference} between two large AEP values, so a \SI{0.5}{\percent} error in either optimization can produce a \SI{50}{\percent} error in regret.
This applies to any study computing AEP differences between strategies---robust optimization, Pareto analysis, or inter-farm coordination assessments.

\subsection{Wind rose shape controls design regret}\label{sec:shape}

Figure~\ref{fig:heatmap} shows design regret across the $(a, f)$ space at $K = 500$.
Regret ranges from \SI{17.2}{GWh} (\SI{0.4}{\percent} of AEP) at $a = 0.5$, $f = 0.0$ to \SI{100.6}{GWh} (\SI{2.5}{\percent}) at $a = 0.9$, $f = 1.0$---a six-fold range.

The folding parameter $f$ is the dominant factor (Fig.~\ref{fig:regret_vs_f}).
Mean regret increases monotonically from \SI{45.2}{GWh} at $f = 0$ to \SI{77.9}{GWh} at $f = 1$.
Concentration ($a$) has a weaker, non-monotonic effect.

Unidirectional wind ($f = 1$) creates a single wake corridor that the liberal layout is blind to; the conservative optimizer shifts turbines laterally to escape it.
Under bidirectional conditions ($f = 0$), wakes arrive from opposing directions and no lateral shift helps---regret is inherently limited.

For North Sea planning, this means that sites with strongly unidirectional southwesterly winds (characteristic of the German Bight and southern North Sea) face higher design regret than sites with more bidirectional conditions (such as Danish waters).
The DEI wind rose ($a \approx 0.64$, $f \approx 0.38$) falls in the moderate-regret region, consistent with the negligible tradeoffs found by \citet{Quick2026omae}.

Figure~\ref{fig:buffer} shows regret vs.\ buffer distance at $K = 500$ for three wind roses.

For concentrated unidirectional wind ($a = 0.9$, $f = 1.0$), regret drops from \SI{2.5}{\percent} at $2D$ to \SI{0.4}{\percent} at $40D$ (\SI{9.6}{km}).
The bidirectional case ($a = 0.5$, $f = 0.0$) shows gentle decay from \SI{0.4}{\percent} to \SI{0.2}{\percent}.

The NYSERDA \SI{4}{nmi} ($\sim$$31D$) recommendation reduces regret to $<$\SI{1}{\percent} for all wind roses tested, but is unnecessarily conservative for bidirectional sites.
Wind-rose-dependent buffer standards could allow denser development at low-risk sites (bidirectional, e.g., DEI) while maintaining protection at high-risk sites (unidirectional, e.g., German Bight).
This is directly relevant to the emerging cross-border coordination frameworks for the North Sea \citep{Fliegner2025}.

\subsection{The ambush effect: directional vulnerability}\label{sec:cross-sections}

Figure~\ref{fig:cross-section} presents regret cross-sections at $K = 500$ (converged) for four elliptical wind roses spanning the shape space.

Peak bearings are unchanged from K=100 to K=500 for three cases and shift slightly (from \SI{135}{\degree} to \SI{105}{\degree}) for the mid-range case (Table~\ref{tab:cross-section}).

Peak regret consistently occurs at oblique angles to the dominant wind (\SI{270}{\degree}), not along it.
We term this the ``ambush effect'': the liberal layout is pre-adapted to the dominant direction and therefore partially resilient to aligned neighbors.
Off-axis neighbors exploit the layout's blind spots---directions the liberal optimizer did not prioritize.

For North Sea cluster planning, this finding is counterintuitive: a developer might assume the most dangerous neighbor is the one directly upwind.
The ambush effect shows that neighbors at oblique angles---perhaps in an adjacent country's lease area at a different bearing---may cause greater design regret.
This is relevant for cross-border coordination in the North Sea, where multiple national lease areas surround each farm at various bearings.

All six cross-sections are fully converged at $K = 500$.
The DEI real wind rose shows peak regret of \SI{143.6}{GWh} (\SI{2.59}{\percent}) at bearing \SI{225}{\degree} and $15D$---consistent with the ``ambush effect,'' as the DEI prevailing wind is from the southwest ($\theta_\text{prev} \approx 251°$) and peak regret occurs \SI{26}{\degree} south of the dominant direction.

\subsection{Self-interested neighbor comparison}

The cross-section analyses above use a fixed reference farm translated to each position.
To test whether the ambush effect persists under realistic neighbor behavior, we additionally optimize the reference farm to maximize its \emph{own} AEP (given the target at its liberal layout) before re-optimizing the target farm.
This self-interested variant represents a neighbor that acts rationally for its own production, not adversarially against the target.

Figure~\ref{fig:si-comparison} compares the adversarial and self-interested cross-sections at $K = 500$ for three wind roses spanning the shape space.
The results reveal a striking wind-rose dependence:
\begin{itemize}
    \item Under bidirectional conditions ($a = 0.5$, $f = 0.0$), the self-interested neighbor produces \SI{60}{\percent} of the adversarial peak regret (\SI{1.45}{\percent} vs.\ \SI{2.42}{\percent} of AEP). The adversarial upper bound substantially overstates the realistic risk.
    \item Under mid-range conditions ($a = 0.7$, $f = 0.5$), a similar reduction occurs: \SI{63}{\percent} of adversarial (\SI{2.01}{\percent} vs.\ \SI{3.21}{\percent}).
    \item Under concentrated unidirectional conditions ($a = 0.9$, $f = 1.0$), the self-interested neighbor produces \emph{nearly double} the adversarial peak regret: \SI{5.99}{\percent} vs.\ \SI{3.24}{\percent} of AEP.
\end{itemize}

The last result is the most policy-relevant: when wind is strongly unidirectional, a neighbor optimizing its own AEP concentrates its turbines upwind---exactly where the strongest wind is, and exactly where they cause the most damage to the target.
The fixed reference grid, by contrast, spreads turbines evenly across its footprint, diluting the wake impact.
This means that for concentrated unidirectional sites, the adversarial upper bound \emph{underestimates} the design regret from a rational neighbor.

For North Sea planning, this implies that sites in the German Bight with strongly unidirectional southwesterly winds face \emph{higher} design regret from realistic neighbor behavior than from adversarial placement---the ``wind theft'' problem is worse than the worst case suggests.

\section{Discussion}\label{sec:discussion}

\subsection{Real North Sea sites in the shape space}

To ground the parametric results in real planning decisions, we fit the elliptical model to five North Sea offshore wind sites (Table~\ref{tab:sites}, Fig.~\ref{fig:heatmap}).
All five sites fall in the moderate-regret region of the $(a, f)$ space, with $a \in [0.62, 0.73]$ and $f \in [0.18, 0.54]$.
No operational North Sea site in our sample reaches the high-regret corner ($a > 0.8$, $f > 0.75$), though this does not preclude future lease areas---particularly in the southern North Sea---from doing so.

\subsection{Implications for North Sea cluster development}

The six-fold variation in design regret across wind rose shape has direct implications for the North Sea's emerging cluster framework.

\paragraph{Economic magnitude.}
For a representative \SI{750}{MW} farm (\num{50}$\times$\SI{15}{MW}), design regret of \SI{2.5}{\percent} corresponds to $\sim$\SI{100}{GWh/yr}.
At a contract-for-difference strike price of \SI{70}{EUR/MWh}, this amounts to $\sim$\SI{7}{M EUR/yr}, or $\sim$\SI{175}{M EUR} over a 25-year operational lifetime.
Even at the moderate regret levels observed for real North Sea sites ($\sim$\SI{1}{\percent}), the lifetime cost exceeds \SI{70}{M EUR}---comparable to the cost of a single turbine foundation.
By contrast, the marginal cost of running a neighbor-aware layout optimization is negligible relative to project CAPEX.

\paragraph{German Bight and southern North Sea.}
Sites with strongly unidirectional southwesterly winds sit in the high-regret region ($f \gtrsim 0.75$).
Here, the value of coordinated design is highest, and buffer distances of $30$--$40D$ (\SIrange{7}{10}{km}) may be needed to reduce regret below \SI{1}{\percent}.
The projected \SI{30}{\percent} capacity factor reduction from cluster wakes \citep{Fliegner2025} is partially a design problem, not just a physics problem: some of this loss could be mitigated by neighbor-aware layout optimization.

\paragraph{Danish waters.}
The DEI wind rose ($f \approx 0.38$) benefits from a natural resilience: wakes from any single direction are partially offset by energy captured from the opposing direction, limiting the rearrangement advantage of a neighbor-aware design.
This explains the negligible design tradeoffs found by \citet{Quick2026omae}.
However, this resilience is a property of the wind climate, not the farm---other Danish sites with different wind characteristics may not share it.

\paragraph{Cross-border coordination.}
The value of inter-farm coordination is itself wind-rose-dependent.
Our results suggest that coordination frameworks like the ``good neighbour'' principle in the UK EN-3 guidance should be weighted by wind rose directionality.
Fixed buffer distances---such as the NYSERDA \SI{4}{nmi} recommendation---are adequate for all sites tested but inefficient: bidirectional sites need only $\sim$$10D$ to achieve the same residual regret.
Wind-rose-conditional buffers could allow denser development at low-risk sites without compromising high-risk ones.

\subsection{The convergence problem}

Our most broadly applicable finding is methodological.
At $K = 5$, regret estimates can be off by nearly a factor of two.
The cause is catastrophic cancellation: regret is a difference between two large, noisy AEP values.
A \SI{0.5}{\percent} miss in either optimization can produce a \SI{50}{\percent} error in the difference.

This affects any study computing AEP differences between strategies.
We recommend $K \geq 300$ for 50-turbine farms and proportionally more for larger farms.
The computational cost at $K = 500$ was approximately \SI{2}{hours} per wind rose on a single AMD MI250X GCD, or \SI{70}{GPU-hours} for the full 35-case sweep.

\subsection{Limitations}

\paragraph{Wake model.}
We use \citet{Bastankhah2014} with $k = 0.04$ and root-sum-of-squares superposition.
More sophisticated models (e.g., TurbOPark) may change absolute magnitudes.
The qualitative findings---folding dominance, the ambush effect---depend on wake corridor geometry, not magnitude, and are expected to be robust.

\paragraph{Adversarial vs.\ self-interested.}
The greedy grid search and fixed reference farm provide an adversarial framing.
The self-interested comparison (Section~\ref{sec:results}) shows that the adversarial upper bound overestimates regret for bidirectional sites (\SI{60}{\percent} of adversarial) but \emph{underestimates} it for concentrated unidirectional sites (1.85$\times$ adversarial).
This asymmetry means the qualitative ordering of sites by risk is robust, but absolute magnitudes depend on neighbor behavior.

\paragraph{Constant wind speed.}
Real North Sea sites have speed--direction correlations.
If high-speed winds are concentrated in the dominant direction (common for storm-driven sites), regret could be amplified through the cubic power relationship.

\paragraph{Single farm geometry.}
Results may depend on the DEI polygon shape.
Different boundaries could affect the layout optimization landscape and the potential for lateral escape from wake corridors.

\paragraph{Cross-section resolution.}
The cross-section analysis uses 24 bearings and 7 distances, which may miss fine angular structure in the regret field.


\conclusions

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Standard multistart practice drastically underestimates design regret.}
    Five random starts capture only \SIrange{55}{86}{\percent} of converged regret.
    All configurations stabilize by $K = 300$, with no change through $K = 2000$.
    Any study computing AEP differences between strategies should use $K \geq 300$.

    \item \textbf{Design regret varies six-fold across the wind rose shape space.}
    At $K = 500$, regret ranges from \SI{17}{GWh/yr} (\SI{0.4}{\percent}) under bidirectional conditions to \SI{101}{GWh/yr} (\SI{2.5}{\percent}) under concentrated unidirectional conditions.
    Folding ($f$, bidirectional vs.\ unidirectional) is the primary driver.

    \item \textbf{Buffer distance requirements are wind-rose-dependent.}
    Concentrated unidirectional sites retain \SI{0.4}{\percent} residual regret at $40D$ (\SI{9.6}{km}).
    Bidirectional sites achieve comparable levels at $10D$ (\SI{2.4}{km}).
    Wind-rose-conditional buffer standards could allow denser North Sea development at low-risk sites.

    \item \textbf{Peak vulnerability occurs at oblique angles to the dominant wind.}
    The ``ambush effect'' means that off-axis neighbors---perhaps from adjacent countries' lease areas---may cause greater design regret than directly upwind neighbors.
    This is relevant for cross-border coordination in the North Sea.
\end{enumerate}

The Danish Energy Island, with its partially bidirectional wind rose ($f \approx 0.38$), sits in the low-to-moderate regret region.
Sites in the southern North Sea with strongly unidirectional southwesterly winds are substantially more vulnerable.
As North Sea clustering intensifies toward the \SI{300}{GW} target, incorporating wind rose directionality into buffer distance standards and coordination frameworks will become increasingly important for efficient maritime spatial planning.
